The Effect of Group Size on Group Cooperation

Paul Wanjohi

Introduction

This study investigates the effects group size has on the evolution of cooperation. The research objective of this study is to empirically investigate the prediction of Reeve and HÃ¶lldobler's model that larger group sizes exhibit higher cooperation levels than smaller ones. An artificial life experiment using an Agent Based System was carried out to perform this investigation. It involved two groups with different initial group sizes and an environment with a fixed resource patchiness value (designed to fix the distribution of resources in the environment). The groups of agents would simulate the foraging activity and emerging behaviour would be observed and recorded.

The measure of cooperation throughout this study was defined as the fitness value assigned to a group. This fitness value incorporates the average within-group cooperation values of the members of the group and the between-group cooperation of the group as a whole.

Research Objectives

The primary objective is to determine if larger groups of autonomous agents have higher levels of cooperations than small groups of agents. The null hypothesis and alternate hypothesis (H0 and H1 respectively) are stated below:

H0: Group size has a positive effect on levels of cooperations of a group.
H1: Group size has no effect on levels of cooperations of a group.

Experiment Description

The study conducted 5 experiments with different initial group sizes. These experiments tested a group with 2000 initial number of agents against a group with 200 initial number of agents. The four experiments that were conducted fixed the group with 2000 initial number of agents and varied the group with 200 initial number of agents to 400, 600, 800 and 1000 for the four experiments respectively.

Data was collected from the simulation and processed to provide the average fitness of the groups across all generations for all the 5 experiments described.

Conclusions


The experiment results demonstrated that the group of 400 starting size exhibits a significant difference in fitness levels when compared to a group of 2000 starting size. Assumptions made in the experiment were biologically inspired and were used to tackle the design challenges experienced in the experiment.
Observations from the experiment demonstrated the existence of temporary spatial groups of agents that follow each other providing a natural way of grouping agents. Ultimately, the experiment demonstrated that there is evidence to suggest that group size has an effect on the levels of cooperation of the group and thus we cannot reject the null hypothesis (H0).
